Nigerian Politics Quiz

Nigerian politics is a dynamic and often dramatic landscape, shaped by a complex interplay of ethnic diversity, colonial legacies, socio-economic challenges, and evolving democratic institutions. As Africa’s most populous nation and one of its largest economies, Nigeria’s political journey reflects both the promise and the turbulence of post-independence governance. Nigeria operates as a federal presidential republic, meaning power is shared between the central government and 36 individual states. The system is modeled loosely on the U.S. Constitution, with three distinct branches: the Executive Branch, headed by the President who serves as both Head of State and Head of Government; the Legislative Branch, consisting of a bicameral National Assembly with a Senate and House of Representatives; and the Judicial Branch, led by the Supreme Court and supported by lower courts. Elections are held every four years and overseen by the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC), though challenges like vote-buying and electoral violence persist. Major political parties include the All Progressives Congress (APC), currently in power under President Bola Ahmed Tinubu; the People’s Democratic Party (PDP), the main opposition; and the Labour Party (LP), which gained momentum in recent years, especially among youth and urban voters. Nigeria’s ethnic diversity—featuring over 250 groups, with Hausa-Fulani in the North, Yoruba in the South-West, and Igbo in the South-East—plays a significant role in shaping political alliances and voting patterns. Historical events such as military coups, the Nigerian Civil War, and the transition to democracy in 1999 have left lasting impacts on governance and public trust. Key political issues include corruption, insecurity, economic reform, and youth engagement, with movements like #EndSARS highlighting demands for accountability. Despite challenges like weak institutions and voter apathy, Nigeria’s democracy continues to evolve, supported by a vibrant civil society and growing digital activism.

Structure of Government

Nigeria operates as a federal presidential republic, meaning:

  • Executive: Led by the President, who serves as both head of state and government.
  • Legislative: Bicameral National Assembly—Senate and House of Representatives.
  • Judiciary: Independent courts, with the Supreme Court at the top.

Major Political Parties

  • All Progressives Congress (APC): Currently dominant, with President Bola Tinubu at the helm.
  • People’s Democratic Party (PDP): Former ruling party, now the main opposition.
  • Labour Party (LP): Gained traction recently, especially among youth and urban voters.
